# Acting as a Product Manager: Building Klaussa.com

Building a product is one thing. Deciding *what* to build, *why* to build it, and *when* to ship it — that's an entirely different challenge. When I started working on [Klaussa.com](https://klaussa.com), I didn't just write code. I stepped into the role of a product manager, and it changed how I approach building software.

## Why I Took on the PM Role

In small teams and solo projects, there's no luxury of having a dedicated product manager. Someone has to own the product direction — the roadmap, the priorities, the trade-offs. For Klaussa, that someone was me.

I quickly realized that being a good developer doesn't automatically make you a good PM. The skillsets overlap, but the mindset is fundamentally different. As a developer, I think about *how*. As a PM, I had to think about *why* and *for whom*.

## Defining the Vision

Before writing a single line of code, I spent time answering three questions:

1. **What problem are we solving?** — I identified the core pain point that Klaussa would address and made sure it was a real problem, not just a cool idea.
2. **Who are we solving it for?** — Understanding the target user helped shape every decision that followed, from feature scope to UI design.
3. **What does success look like?** — I set clear, measurable goals so I could evaluate whether the product was actually moving in the right direction.

These questions became the foundation for every feature decision and prioritization call down the road.

## Prioritization and Trade-offs

One of the hardest parts of being a PM is saying no. There were countless features I *could* build, but the real skill was identifying which ones I *should* build first.

I used a simple framework to prioritize:

- **Impact vs. Effort** — High-impact, low-effort items shipped first. This kept momentum high and delivered value to users quickly.
- **Must-have vs. Nice-to-have** — I separated core functionality from bells and whistles. The MVP had to be lean but complete enough to validate the idea.
- **User feedback loops** — I talked to potential users early and often, using their input to adjust the roadmap rather than building in a vacuum.

## Wearing Multiple Hats

Acting as a PM while also being the developer meant constantly switching contexts. In the morning, I might be mapping out user flows and writing acceptance criteria. By afternoon, I'd be deep in code implementing those exact features.

This dual role had its advantages. I understood the technical constraints intimately, so I could make realistic commitments. I didn't need lengthy handoff documents — the context lived in my head. But it also had its pitfalls. Sometimes I'd over-engineer a feature because the developer in me wanted to build it "right," when the PM in me should have pushed for shipping it fast and iterating later.

## Lessons Learned

Here are the key takeaways from my experience as a PM on Klaussa:

- **Ship early, iterate often.** Perfection is the enemy of progress. Getting a working version in front of users taught me more than weeks of planning ever could.
- **Data over opinions.** When I had conflicting ideas about which direction to take, I let user behavior and feedback break the tie instead of relying on gut feeling.
- **Document your decisions.** Future-you will thank present-you for writing down *why* you chose one approach over another. It makes revisiting decisions much easier.
- **Scope creep is real.** Every "quick addition" adds up. I learned to guard the scope ruthlessly and push non-essential features to future iterations.
- **Talk to your users.** This one sounds obvious, but it's easy to skip when you're busy building. Regular conversations with actual users kept me grounded and focused on what mattered.

## What's Next

Klaussa is still evolving, and so is my approach to product management. I'm continuing to refine the roadmap based on real-world usage and feedback. The PM hat never really comes off — it just becomes a more natural part of how I think about building products.

If you're a developer thinking about taking on the PM role in your own project, my advice is simple: start with the user, stay disciplined about scope, and don't be afraid to ship something imperfect. You'll learn more from a launched product than from a perfect plan that never sees the light of day.
